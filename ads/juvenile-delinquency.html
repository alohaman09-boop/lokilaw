What To Do Right Now

If your child was just arrested or charged with a juvenile delinquency offense in Montana, the next 24–48 hours matter more than most parents realize. Here's exactly what you need to do before anything else.

1

Tell Your Child To Remain Silent Until a Lawyer Is Present

Anything your child says — to police, school officials, or investigators — can and will be used against them in juvenile court. Instruct your child clearly: be polite, but say nothing about what happened until a juvenile delinquency attorney is with them. This constitutional right applies to minors in Montana.

2

Do Not Allow Questioning Without You AND an Attorney Present

This applies to police interrogations, school disciplinary investigations, and any other official questioning. Even well-meaning school administrators can create a record that damages your child's juvenile delinquency case. You have the right to have a lawyer present — exercise it every time.

3

Gather All Paperwork From the Arrest, School, or Court

Collect everything — citations, detention notices, court summons, school disciplinary letters, and any written communication from law enforcement. Bring all of it to your first consultation. Juvenile court deadlines move fast and missing them can severely limit your child's options.

4

Keep Your Child and Family Completely Off Social Media

No posts, comments, stories, or messages about the incident from anyone in the family — including your child, siblings, or friends. Social media content is regularly used as evidence in juvenile delinquency proceedings and can significantly damage a defense. Silence online is protection.

5

The earlier a juvenile delinquency lawyer gets involved, the more options your family has. Early intervention can mean the difference between diversion and a formal record, between probation and detention. The free consultation costs nothing — waiting can cost your child everything.

Frequently Asked Questions

Parents come to us with the same urgent questions about juvenile delinquency in Montana. Here are the most common ones — answered honestly.

In Montana, "juvenile delinquency" refers to criminal acts committed by a minor — generally a person under the age of 18. When a minor commits an act that would be a crime if committed by an adult, it is classified as a delinquent act under Montana law and is handled through the Youth Court system. Juvenile delinquency is legally and procedurally distinct from adult criminal court. The juvenile justice system in Montana places a formal emphasis on rehabilitation rather than punishment — but that does not mean the consequences are minor. A juvenile delinquency adjudication can result in probation, mandatory counseling, fines, community service, detention in a youth facility, and a delinquency record that can affect your child's future. Understanding what your child is facing — and getting experienced legal counsel early — is essential.

When a minor is charged with a crime in Montana, the case is typically referred to Youth Court rather than adult criminal court. From there, the case may be handled informally — through a diversion program or informal supervision — or formally, through a petition filed in Youth Court. Informal resolution avoids a formal delinquency adjudication and is often the best outcome for a first-time offender. Formal proceedings can result in probation, mandatory treatment programs, community service, restitution, or placement in a youth correctional facility. For the most serious offenses, a prosecutor may seek to have the minor tried as an adult — a process called transfer or certification — which carries dramatically more severe consequences. The outcome depends heavily on the charges, your child's history, and the quality of their legal representation. Early intervention by a juvenile delinquency lawyer gives your family the most options.

Yes — Montana law allows for the expungement of juvenile delinquency records in certain circumstances, and planning for expungement is one of the most important reasons to have an attorney actively managing your child's case from the very beginning. Generally, a juvenile may petition for expungement after reaching age 18, completing all terms of their sentence or probation, and waiting a specified period without any further offenses. Once expunged, the record is sealed and does not need to be disclosed on most job applications, college admissions forms, or standard background checks. However, expungement is not automatic — it requires a formal petition and a judge's order. The path to expungement starts with how the original juvenile delinquency case is handled. An attorney who is thinking about your child's long-term future from day one — not just the next hearing — makes a real difference in whether expungement becomes a realistic option.

Montana Youth Court strongly prefers community-based alternatives to secure detention, particularly for first-time offenders and non-violent delinquency charges.

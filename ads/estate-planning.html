Getting Started

Five Simple Steps to Begin Your Estate Plan

Estate planning doesn't have to be complicated. Here's how to start thinking through it before your first conversation with us.

1

Take Stock of Your Major Assets

Make a list of what you own — your home, vehicles, retirement accounts, bank accounts, investment accounts, and life insurance policies. Knowing what you have is the foundation of any estate plan.

2

Identify Your Decision-Makers

Think about who you trust to handle your financial and medical decisions if you're ever unable to do so yourself. This is the person named in your power of attorney documents — it's a significant responsibility.

3

Think Through Who Inherits What

Consider who you want to receive your property and in what proportions. Think beyond the obvious — what if a beneficiary passes before you? Having a plan for secondary beneficiaries matters.

4

Consider Whether a Will or Trust Fits Better

A simple will works for many families. A living trust may be a better fit if you own real estate, have a blended family, or want to avoid probate. We'll walk you through the differences — no pressure, just clarity.

You don't need to have it all figured out before calling. That's what the consultation is for. We'll listen, answer your questions, and help you build a plan that reflects your values and protects the people you love.

It directs where your property goes, names an executor to manage your estate, and — critically — designates a guardian for any minor children. A revocable living trust does more: it allows your estate to bypass the Montana probate process entirely, provides privacy, and can offer more nuanced control over how and when assets are distributed. If you own real estate, have a larger estate, have a blended family, or simply want to make things as easy as possible for your heirs, a trust is often the better choice. We'll help you figure out which combination fits your life.

A durable power of attorney (DPOA) is a legal document that authorizes a person you choose — called your agent or attorney-in-fact — to manage your financial affairs if you become incapacitated. "Durable" means the document remains effective even if you lose mental capacity, which is when it matters most. Without a DPOA in place, your family may need to go through a court-supervised guardianship or conservatorship proceeding just to pay your bills or manage your accounts — a time-consuming and expensive process that a simple document can prevent. A separate healthcare power of attorney designates someone to make medical decisions on your behalf.

If you die without a will in Montana, your estate passes according to Montana's intestate succession laws — a one-size-fits-all formula that distributes property to relatives in a fixed order, regardless of your wishes, your relationships, or your family's actual needs. A surviving spouse and children share the estate under a statutory formula, which may not reflect what you actually wanted. More significantly, if you have minor children and no will, a court — not you — decides who raises them. Dying intestate also means your estate almost certainly goes through probate, which is public, time-consuming, and costly. A properly drafted will resolves all of this.

For some very simple situations, a basic online will may be better than nothing. But the real danger with DIY estate planning services is what they don't cover — Montana-specific requirements, proper execution and witnessing formalities, trust funding (a trust that isn't properly funded is essentially useless), beneficiary coordination with retirement accounts and life insurance, and the edge cases that no questionnaire can anticipate. People often discover the shortcomings of a generic template at the worst possible moment — during probate, when a trust fails to include an asset, or when a power of attorney is rejected by a financial institution.
